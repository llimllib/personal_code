couple recommendations (none / 0) (#12)
by SocratesGhost on Thu May 27th, 2004 at 05:23:22 PM EST

Few employers nowadays will pay for relocation, especially in California where there is a glut on almost every type of programmer. Most employers will see your out of state address and phone number and immediately eliminate you on this basis alone. If you can relocate yourself before submitting your resume, this is best. Alternatively, if you have any friends in the area, use their contact info as your contact info so it makes you appear local. Just be ready to travel quickly for an interview.

Do not lie to your future employer about where you really are, but indicate that you're using a friend's house as a base of operation until you move yourself. This way, you've made it past the relocation hurdle. You never know, they may be amenable to giving a relocation package especially if you've specialized in some sort of niche.

If you know the area to which you're going to move, start getting the local paper as often as you can. Libraries can be helpful in this way. I find that online classifieds are still behind the print ads for jobs.

A moving tip: sell your car. Buy an old school bus. I'm not kidding. They are cheaper to buy than you'd expect and then after you reach your destination, you can sell it usually for a profit. I know 3 people who came to San Diego in this manner, including one guy who drove it to work for about 2 months until he had a buyer.

Did it, worked out great. (none / 3) (#7)
by undermyne on Thu May 27th, 2004 at 04:16:58 PM EST
(undermyne at gmail dot com)

Submit resume's now and try to line up interviews before you go, when I came down from canada I had 3 interviews for the week that I arrived. See dice.com for job listings. For the west coast, it is THE job board regardless of what anyone tells you.

Drive your car out, stay at hostels or KOA campgrounds on your way out.

When you get where your going, find a hostel to stay in while you get established. Depending on where you choose to go, KOA campgrounds would work too. Be prepared to stay for for a couple months (may not take that long).

When you get where you are going, find an evening job at a place where you can get free food (restaurant), do not mention said employment in job interviews.

Don't be a stickler on pay, its an employers market still. Get your foot in the door and work your way up either at that company or parlay that experience into a new job (give two weeks, only quit after the new job is in place).

Happy hunting!

A few options (none / 3) (#5)
by the dehorned unicorn on Thu May 27th, 2004 at 03:55:13 PM EST

1) Save up in advance. Get a job where you are; aim to be there a year tops. Spend no dime you don't have to. You'll probably want to have $3000 ; but with luck you can make do with less. When you've got the money, you've got enough for an apartment and food, and bare necessities for 2-3 months; spend all your effort finding a job after you've got an apartment. It's probably best to get a 3 month lease, since you might want to move to be closer to work, or move once you're more familiar with the area.

2) Get a job. Start applying out west until you get someone who'll give you a job. Places are less likely to want to interview people from further away, but college grads are a bit of an exception. Especially since you're coming from college the job might pay to relocate you. Note; they won't pay ahead, they'll reimburse you. Remember, it might be as much as 4 weeks from when they sign you before you see a cent.

I did number 2. This was just before the dot bomb crash, so while I found a place who was willing to reimburse my plane fair (they hired me on friday to start on monday, with phone interviews only... they were desperate for a sysadmin), and I even got them to agree to reimburse me within a week. I had 1500, and I was lucky enough to know someone in the area who let me crash at his place. However, $300 for the plane ticket; money to rent a car (which later was reimbursed); gas driving 60 miles round trip; $800 first months rent $500 security deposit. I'm lucky I had room on my credit card when I made this jump; or I'd have been short being able to get the money together to get the place I did.

Either way you look at it, in order to have a chance at being successful, you'll need a non-trivial amount of money, so unless you know of a place who's willing to do everything to get you out there (back in the day, MS would handle plane tickets/car rental/and set you up in a hotel for a month so you wouldn't have to do out of pocket and later get compensated), you should get a loan from your parents, or start working where you are.
